## üîç What Is The Mom Test?

The Mom Test, by Rob Fitzpatrick, teaches you to validate business ideas through customer conversations that even your mom can't lie about. The core principle: **Talk about their life, not your idea.**

### The Three Rules:
1. **Talk about their life, not your idea** - Focus on their current problems and behaviors
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future** - "When was the last time you..." not "Would you use..."
3. **Talk less, listen more** - You're gathering data, not pitching

### Why This Matters For You:
Your pitch document (PITCH_PREPARATION.md) is extremely well-crafted for **selling**, but shows no evidence of customer discovery conversations. You need to validate assumptions BEFORE you start selling.

## üí° The Mom Test Applied to Your Product

### ‚ùå Bad Questions (What NOT to Ask):

**DON'T ASK:**
- "Would you use a tool that turns 3 hours of Excel work into 30 seconds?" *(Leading question, everyone says yes)*
- "Do you think this would be valuable?" *(Opinion about hypothetical future)*
- "How much would you pay for this?" *(They'll lowball or give polite answers)*
- "Would this solve your pallet loss problem?" *(They're trying to make you feel good)*

**Why These Are Bad:**
- They allow people to lie to make you feel good
- They collect opinions, not facts
- They focus on your solution, not their problem
- They get hypothetical answers that don't predict real behavior

### ‚úÖ Good Questions (What TO Ask):

**ASK THESE INSTEAD:**

#### **About Current Workflow:**
- "Walk me through what you did yesterday morning when you arrived at work."
- "When was the last time you had to hunt for a lost pallet? Tell me what happened."
- "Show me your Excel file. How long does this typically take you to review?"
- "What tools are you currently using to track this? Can I see your screen?"

#### **About Pain Intensity:**
- "When was the last time a pallet got lost? What was the cost?"
- "How many times this month have you gotten that 'where's pallet X?' radio call?"
- "What happens when you DON'T find the issue in time? Tell me about a specific example."
- "What's the worst pallet loss incident you've personally experienced?"

#### **About Current Solutions:**
- "What have you tried to solve this problem? What happened?"
- "Are you currently paying for any software to help with this? How much?"
- "What would need to happen for your company to approve a new software purchase?"
- "Who else in your organization worries about this problem?"

#### **About Budget & Authority:**
- "When was the last time your warehouse bought software? What was the process?"
- "If you found a solution tomorrow, what would need to happen to get it approved?"
- "What's your annual budget for operational software?"
- "Who has to sign off on purchases like this?"

#### **About Priorities:**
- "Of all the problems you face in the warehouse, where does this rank?"
- "If you had an extra $10K in budget, what would you spend it on first?"
- "What are you currently measured on? How is your performance evaluated?"

**Why These Are Good:**
- They get specific stories about real events
- They reveal actual behavior, not hypothetical opinions
- They uncover the real budget/authority structure
- They help you understand if this problem is actually a priority
